What is Ontology?

Ontology is, at its core, the study of what exists and how things are related.

Depending on whether you are talking to a philosopher or a computer scientist, the definition shifts from abstract reality to practical data structures.

Here is the breakdown of the two main perspectives:

1. In Philosophy (The Origin)

In philosophy, ontology is the “study of being.” It is a branch of metaphysics that asks deep questions about the nature of reality. This tradition traces back to Aristotle’s Metaphysics and continues in modern analytic philosophy.

  • The Goal: To discover the fundamental categories of existence.
  • Key Questions: What does it mean “to exist”? Do abstract ideas (like the number 7 or “justice”) exist in the same way a rock exists? This is often referred to as Ontological Commitment.
  • Example: A philosopher might analyze if “holes” are real things, or if they are just the absence of something else.

2. In Computer Science (The Application)

In computer science (specifically AI and Information Science), an ontology is a formal blueprint used to model knowledge. It is a specific way of organizing data so that computers can “understand” it.

The most widely cited definition comes from Tom Gruber, who defined an ontology as a “formal, explicit specification of a shared conceptualization”.

Instead of asking “what is reality,” a computational ontology declares: “For this specific system, these are the only things that exist, and here is how they connect.”

  • The Goal: To help software share data, reason about information, and avoid confusion (Interoperability).
  • The “Triples” Structure: Most computational ontologies (like those built with the W3C Web Ontology Language (OWL)) are built on three main components:
    1. Classes (The Nouns): The categories of things (e.g., Patient, Doctor, Drug).
    2. Attributes (The Adjectives): The properties of those things (e.g., Name, Dosage, Blood Type).
    3. Relationships (The Verbs): How they interact (e.g., Doctor prescribes Drug).

Summary Comparison

Feature Philosophical Ontology Computational Ontology
Focus The nature of reality. The structure of a specific domain’s data.
Goal Truth and wisdom. Interoperability (systems talking to each other).
Output Theories and essays. Knowledge Graphs, code (OWL/RDF), databases.
Example “Is the mind separate from the body?” “A Smartphone is-a Device.”

Why is this important right now?

You likely interact with ontologies every day without realizing it: * Google Search: When you search for “Jaguar,” Google uses a Knowledge Graph (an ontology) to figure out if you mean the animal, the car, or the football team. * Healthcare: Ontologies allow different hospitals to share records. One system might say “Heart Attack” and another “Myocardial Infarction,” but the ontology links them as the exact same concept (see SNOMED CT).
